Frequently Asked Questions

Monthly payment is calculated using the standard amortization formula: M = P × [r(1+r)ⁿ] / [(1+r)ⁿ−1], where P = principal, r = monthly interest rate (annual rate ÷ 12), and n = total number of payments. Each payment covers that month's interest first, with the remainder reducing the principal balance.
An amortization schedule is a table showing each payment over the life of a loan, broken down into how much goes toward interest vs. principal. Early payments are mostly interest; later payments are mostly principal. This is why paying extra early in a loan saves so much in total interest.
On a $300,000, 30-year mortgage, a 1% rate difference (e.g., 6% vs 7%) changes your monthly payment by about $195/month and the total interest paid by roughly $70,000 over the life of the loan. Rate differences matter enormously on large, long-term loans.
Mortgage rates vary based on credit score, loan type, down payment, and market conditions. As of early 2026, 30-year fixed rates have generally been in the 6–7% range. Always compare rates from multiple lenders and consider getting pre-approved to see your personalized rate.
A 15-year mortgage has higher monthly payments but significantly less total interest paid — often 50–60% less. A 30-year mortgage gives lower monthly payments and more cash flow flexibility, but you pay much more in interest over time. Use this calculator to compare both scenarios side by side.
